Conduit · what we built · phase one

An enterprise AI gateway.

One question in. One grounded, entitled, explainable answer out.

You ask a question in plain language. Conduit works out which of your systems can answer, checks that you're allowed to see what they return, and gives you one answer grounded in real data — with the full decision visible. We built it because the obvious approach, putting a language model in front of your data and letting people chat with it, is not safe for a serious business. A chatbot answers. Conduit is accountable. That distinction is the whole of this document.

01 · Why it isn't a chatbot

A chatbot answers. That is exactly the problem.

A chatbot is built to continue the sentence. Ask it anything and it produces fluent text at the same confident cadence whether it is right, wrong, or forbidden — because it has no way to tell those apart. For a demo, that's dazzling. For a regulated enterprise, it's a liability, in three specific ways.

✕It doesn't know what you're allowed to see.

Ask a wrapped model about a client and it will tell you — whether or not that client is yours to discuss. Conduit gates every answer by who is asking, at every step.

✕It invents numbers.

A model produces figures that are the right shape for the sentence, not values from a system of record. Conduit never lets the model compute or recall a number; every figure traces to real data a system actually returned.

✕It can't show its work.

Ask why it said what it said and a chatbot has nothing — the reasoning evaporated the moment it was generated. Conduit shows the whole decision, live, and keeps a record.

A chatbot answers. Conduit is accountable.

None of these is something you can bolt onto a chatbot later. They're consequences of building the system, from the start, to stand behind its answers rather than just produce them.

02 · What we built

A gateway that carries no business knowledge.

Conduit is a single gateway service that sits between the people asking questions and the systems that hold the answers. It does five things, and only five: understand the question, route it to the right systems, check entitlements, call those systems in parallel, and synthesize one grounded answer — with every step observable.

The important part is what the gateway does not contain. It carries no business knowledge. It doesn't know what a client is, or what an insurance policy looks like, or that your business has the domains it has. All of that lives outside the gateway, as configuration. The gateway is a general-purpose engine; the business is described to it, per request, and it acts on that description. We'll come back to why that matters more than anything else here.

Phase one is real and running: one gateway, 13 specialist agents across 4 business domains, verified end to end.

03 · One question, end to end

Follow a real question through the machine.

Take a question a real user asks — "how exposed is the Meridian relationship across our books?" — and watch what happens to it.

1
Understand
read the question, extract the references
2
Route
match meaning to the right systems
3
Entitle
is this person allowed?
4
Fan out
call the systems in parallel
5
Synthesize
one grounded answer, shown live

It reads the question, but it never guesses. The model notices that "Meridian" is a reference to a client — and stops there. It does not decide which client; a model guessing an identity is exactly what we don't allow. Deterministic code resolves "Meridian" against the real records. If the reference is ambiguous or missing, the system asks — "which Meridian?" — rather than picking one.

A bank that guesses is a casino. So the machine asks.

It routes by meaning. The gateway matches the question against what each agent says it can do, and calls the ones that fit — in parallel. A narrow question hits one agent; a broad one, like this, fans out across domains.

It survives failure. If an agent times out, the others continue. The gateway takes what came back, notes what didn't, and the answer says so — "…and the custody position could not be retrieved" — instead of hiding the gap. A partial answer, labeled as partial, is worth more to a professional than a whole one that might be quietly missing a piece.

It writes the answer from real data. After the entitlement check (next), the model does one job: summarize what the systems returned into plain English. It doesn't compute, recall, or invent. Every step sits in a live trace beside the answer.

04 · Entitlements

"Are you allowed to see this?" is not one question.

In an enterprise it's several — independent, each asking something different about who you are relative to this specific data. Conduit checks all of them, and the answer is released only if every one passes.

01
Segment
Which part of the business you belong to — wealth vs. insurance.
02
Classification
How sensitive the data is — a ladder from internal up to confidential-PII — and how high you're cleared.
03
Coverage
Whether this specific client is in your book of business.
04
Audience
Whether a given system is even offered to someone like you.
05
Role
What your role permits — an auditor can see broadly but change nothing.
06
Tenant
Hard isolation between separate institutions on the same platform.
07
Access mode
Whether you may read the data, or also change it.

One design choice matters most. The gateway finds the client for everyone, identically, and then decides whether you're allowed to hear about it. Existence is universal; disclosure is gated. That separation is what makes the access controls auditable — a wall you can prove, not an accident of how a query happened to be written.

05 · How it grows

A new capability is a file, not a release.

Everything above, the gateway does generically — over a description supplied from outside. That description is a manifest, and it's the whole reason the gateway can carry no business knowledge and still serve any business.

// an agent, entire — a description the gateway reads, not code it integrates
{
  "agent_id":     "acme.wealth.holdings",
  "capability":   "portfolio positions & allocation for a client",
  "examples": [                // how the router finds it, by meaning
    "what are the current holdings for this client",
    "portfolio allocation and total value" ],
  "protocol":     "http",         // http or mcp — the gateway calls both the same way
  "url":          "http://wealth-service:8081",
  "constraints": { "access_mode": "read", "data_classification": "confidential-pii" }
}
Add a capability → one entry. Add a whole business line → one file. The gateway is never changed.

An agent is a short JSON file: an id, a plain description of what it does, a few example questions (which are how the router finds it), the protocol it speaks, the address of the system that holds its data, and its constraints. That's the whole of it — a description, not a module.

So adding a capability is one entry, and adding an entire new business line is one file. The gateway is never modified, because there's nothing business-specific inside it to modify.

Configuration, not code. When we added a fourth domain, the gateway's code did not change at all.

06 · It isn't a banking system

We proved it in a bank. It doesn't know it's in a bank.

We built and proved this in a bank, because a bank is about the hardest place to earn trust — strict entitlements, real regulation, no tolerance for an invented number. But nothing inside the gateway knows it is in a bank. Point the same system at a hospital's records, an insurer's claims, a law firm's matters, a government's filings, and nothing inside it changes, because there was never anything bank-specific inside it to change.

Banking is where we proved it. It isn't what it is.

That's the payoff of a gateway that carries no business knowledge: the accountability — entitlements, grounding, the visible decision — is the same for every industry, and each business brings its own domain as configuration. What we built is not a banking machine. It's an accountable one, and accountability is industry-neutral.

07 · Where it goes

The engine is built. What's left is to point it at more of the enterprise.

Phase one answered the hard question: can an AI system be genuinely accountable — entitled, grounded, explainable — and still be easy to extend? It can, and it's running. From here the same foundation grows by adding manifests, not rewriting the core: more domains, more capabilities, more of the business brought online without touching the gateway.

The bar we set was simple — every answer entitled, grounded, and explainable, and the system easy to grow. That's what we built.
